## Page 02 — Foreword, 1 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [sch-tit-05.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/schpref/schpref02.html)</sub>

Besides my two previously published contributions to the enrichment of the Sanskrit dictionary — WZKM 29, 259 ff. and ZDMG 71, 1 ff. — the present glossary contains essentially the yield from the Yaśastilakacampū. Its richness is palpable, so that its printing alone already appears justified. The author, moreover, himself recalls at the close of his work its lexical productivity when he says, II, 418, 20/21:

> *abhidhānanidhāne 'smin Yaśastilakanāmani*
> *Yaśodharamahārājacarite stān matiḥ satām.*

All the more do I regret that I have not been able to work through Somadevasūri's work completely with confidence. The commentary unfortunately breaks off in Volume II, p. 243, l. 8, and without this knowledgeable guide it is impossible to find one's way through the primeval forest with certainty.¹) Indeed, even in the glossed portion much still remains obscure, or at least questionable!

The words, or meanings, or a particular gender wholly lacking in the pw I have again marked, as before, with °, while * indicates that the item in question is not yet attested there. In addition, my list contains a number of vocables without these marks; these are ones that have been added in Böhtlingk's general index and now, as new attestations, should not be missing from my list. The references are deliberately not complete; an "etc" occasionally expresses this in the case of especially frequent vocables. Citation is by volume, page, and line; v. u. means "from below," whereby the lines that contain notes have not been counted. For two-line stanzas I have used a, b, and for four-line ones a, b, c, d, all of which also applies to the remaining texts.

That Galanos again comes into his own in my list I wish to emphasize particularly: *antika, kakubha, kāraṭaka* and *prajava* I have noted as relevant attestations of this kind. But that I have also fully worked through the commentary to the Yaśastilakacampū goes, of course, without saying. Śrutadevasūri was a man very well versed in lexicography, who often enough glosses a rare word of the text with two at least equally rare expressions²) or explains a quite common word such as *śvan* with an unusual one (in this case *saramāsuta*: I, 126, 15). He therefore deserves our full attention. Finds from his commentary I have marked with (Ko.).

---

¹) A new expedition I undertook on 10 April 1922 and brought to a close on 28 May, laden with rich booty.

²) Compare also cases such as *kolikasya tantuvāyasya kuvindasya* I, 126, 10 v. u.

## Page 03 — Foreword, 2 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [sch-tit-06.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/schpref/schpref03.html)</sub>

[page VI]

I must not leave unmentioned the fact that the edition contains a whole number of misprints, which may here and there need to be taken into account in judging my list. One should not, however, hold this too severely against the deserving editors, for they rightly recall the saying which, by the way, I should like to have applied to my own achievement as well:

> *gacchataḥ skhalanaṃ kvāpi bhavaty eva pramādataḥ;*
> *hasanti durjanās tatra, samādadhati sajjanāḥ.*

Now I had originally intended to publish my anthology from the Yaśastilakam on its own; on 7 March 1921 I wrote on the title page of the press-ready manuscript, as still stands, the sigh of relief: Augiae stabulis purgatis plaudite, amici — when my plan took an entirely new direction through the Helioplan reprint of the pw. The publisher, namely, approached me with the request that I supply a supplement to it and, above all, issue an appeal in order to win the support of fellow specialists. It appeared in the LZ, in the issue of 1 August 1923, but unfortunately remained as good as unnoticed, only Geldner, Jolly, Zachariae, and cand. E. Baer (Zürich) coming forward. Strange, most strange! But at least as peculiar was it that, as the firm Markert & Petters informed me, various experts [? ?] in Leipzig were of the opinion that a supplement to the pw, transcribed, would have no success!!! Since I really have no understanding for such wisdom, I broke off the negotiations at once and concluded my contract with the Orient-Buchhandlung, whose proprietor, Herr Heinz Lafaire, has from the outset shown so much accommodation and understanding that I consider it my duty to acknowledge this publicly with deepest gratitude. He also had at once the same thought as I, namely that Böhtlingk's supplements would have to be worked into my list without remainder if our publication was to become something really useful. The time available, however, unfortunately did not nearly suffice to check Böhtlingk's citations or even, what would have been far more desirable, to adapt them to the most recent editions: I simply took everything over and only now and then made small external improvements. For the rest, it will be an easy matter to establish at once in each individual case the right of ownership; where * or ° should not suffice, the citation or its form decides definitively. Moreover, I should not be at all annoyed if articles that come from me were ascribed to Böhtlingk. I have an extraordinarily high regard for the achievement embodied in the pw, and a pretty good share still remains for me. Without violating modesty, I may well state that to the roughly 14450 articles in Böhtlingk's supplements I have about 12000 new, or original, ones to set against them. These derive from the following texts:

Amaru(śatakam) ed. Simon und ed. Kāvyamālā.

Amit(agati), Subhāṣitasaṃdoha ed. Hertel u. Schmidt, ZDMG 59. 61.

Unmattar(āghavam) ed. Kāvyamālā Nr. 17.

E(rotik), Beiträge zur indischen. 1st edition. The letters standing in brackets after it mean: A = Anaṅgaraṅga; D = Dinālāpanikā-Śukasaptati; K = Kandarpacūḍāmaṇi; P = Pañcasāyaka; R = Ratirahasya; Rm = Ratimañjarī; S = Smaradīpikā. Some of the more recent erotic authors have meanwhile appeared in Benares and Lahore; they are cited occasionally.

Kathāk(autukam) my edition, Kiel 1898.

## Page 05 — Foreword, 4 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [sch-tit-08.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/schpref/schpref05.html)</sub>

[page VIII]

My two helpers Zachariae and Baer I recall with particular gratitude. From the former come many new attestations and cross-references, while the latter has contributed over 100 new vocables, or meanings, from texts of Kaśm(iri) Śiv(aism). Their finds are marked with [Z.] resp. [B.].

With appreciation I also recall the typesetter, who dealt with my manuscript unerringly. This was not entirely easy, because a large part of it was jotted down at a time when the paper shortage had risen to its highest, or when what one bought as writing paper would befriend itself with neither pen nor ink. I therefore found myself obliged to use slips of paper that had already been used two or even three times for other works and that in the end were in part so confusingly motley in appearance that only a practiced eye was able to discern the right path.

Finally, one more remark, the omission of which would be against my innermost conviction: I had imagined a new edition of the pw quite differently! It would have been high time to supplement Böhtlingk's work with the help of all the new material that has appeared since 1889. How rich the yield would have been I have seen especially in the *bhāṇa's* that I read, several of which, despite their slight extent, have yielded several hundred nova. But of course, if thorough work is to be accomplished, all the Indologists of the world would have to come together! That this might soon happen is my sincere wish.

Münster (Westphalia), 26 September 1924.

Richard Schmidt.
