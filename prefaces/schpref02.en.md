# Foreword

Besides my two previously published contributions to the enrichment of the Sanskrit dictionary — WZKM 29, 259 ff. and ZDMG 71, 1 ff. — the present glossary contains essentially the yield from the Yaśastilakacampū. Its richness is palpable, so that its printing alone already appears justified. The author, moreover, himself recalls at the close of his work its lexical productivity when he says, II, 418, 20/21:

> *abhidhānanidhāne 'smin Yaśastilakanāmani*
> *Yaśodharamahārājacarite stān matiḥ satām.*

All the more do I regret that I have not been able to work through Somadevasūri's work completely with confidence. The commentary unfortunately breaks off in Volume II, p. 243, l. 8, and without this knowledgeable guide it is impossible to find one's way through the primeval forest with certainty.¹) Indeed, even in the glossed portion much still remains obscure, or at least questionable!

The words, or meanings, or a particular gender wholly lacking in the pw I have again marked, as before, with °, while * indicates that the item in question is not yet attested there. In addition, my list contains a number of vocables without these marks; these are ones that have been added in Böhtlingk's general index and now, as new attestations, should not be missing from my list. The references are deliberately not complete; an "etc" occasionally expresses this in the case of especially frequent vocables. Citation is by volume, page, and line; v. u. means "from below," whereby the lines that contain notes have not been counted. For two-line stanzas I have used a, b, and for four-line ones a, b, c, d, all of which also applies to the remaining texts.

That Galanos again comes into his own in my list I wish to emphasize particularly: *antika, kakubha, kāraṭaka* and *prajava* I have noted as relevant attestations of this kind. But that I have also fully worked through the commentary to the Yaśastilakacampū goes, of course, without saying. Śrutadevasūri was a man very well versed in lexicography, who often enough glosses a rare word of the text with two at least equally rare expressions²) or explains a quite common word such as *śvan* with an unusual one (in this case *saramāsuta*: I, 126, 15). He therefore deserves our full attention. Finds from his commentary I have marked with (Ko.).
